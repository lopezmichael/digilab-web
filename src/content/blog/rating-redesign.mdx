We've completely redesigned how DigiLab calculates player ratings. If you've noticed your number changed — this is why. More importantly, here's what it means for you and the competitive scene.

## TL;DR

- **Your ranking position matters more than the raw number**
- **Everyone's numbers compressed** — it's not that you got worse
- **The new system is fairer** — no more penalty for taking breaks
- **#1 is still #1** — top players stayed at the top

## The Problem with the Old System

The previous rating algorithm had several technical issues that were distorting the leaderboard:

### 1. Time-Based Decay

The old system weighted recent tournaments more heavily. Sounds reasonable, right? The problem: it penalized players who took breaks. Miss a month of locals and watch your rating tank — even if you were dominating when you played.

### 2. Artificial Spread

The decay mechanism was creating a flat, uniform distribution of ratings. In a proper Elo system, you expect to see a bell curve centered around the starting rating (1500). We were seeing a plateau instead — a red flag that something was wrong.

### 3. Tie Handling Bug

Tied placements were being treated as losses (0) instead of draws (0.5). This meant head-to-head ties in swiss rounds were hurting both players instead of being neutral.

## What We Changed

The new algorithm fixes all three issues:

| Issue | Old System | New System |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| Processing | 5-pass iteration (recalculated everything) | Single-pass chronological |
| Time weighting | Recent = more valuable | All results weighted equally |
| Ties | Treated as losses (0) | Treated as draws (0.5) |

## By the Numbers

Here's how the distribution changed:

| Metric | Old Algorithm | New Algorithm |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| Rating Range | 1245 - 1760 (515 spread) | 1364 - 1645 (281 spread) |
| Mean Rating | 1497.9 | 1499.2 |
| Distribution Shape | Flat/Uniform | Bell Curve |

The narrower spread isn't a bug — it's the fix. The old system was artificially inflating the gap between players.

## How Rankings Changed

| Change | Players | Percentage |
|--------|---------|------------|
| Improved 50+ positions | 293 | 26.7% |
| Improved 10-49 positions | 228 | 20.8% |
| Within 10 positions | 125 | 11.4% |
| Dropped 10-49 positions | 206 | 18.8% |
| Dropped 50+ positions | 244 | 22.3% |

**Key insight:** More players improved their rank (47.5%) than dropped (41.1%). The old system was disproportionately boosting players with recent-but-limited results.

## Who Was Most Affected?

### Biggest drops
Players with few events who played recently. They benefited most from the provisional K-factor combined with the recency bonus. Some dropped 50+ positions despite the same match record.

### Biggest gains
Players with older events. Their historical results are no longer penalized by decay. Some gained 100+ rating points just from having their past wins count equally.

## The Simple Analogy

> The old system gave bonus points for playing recently, which artificially inflated active players and penalized those who took breaks. The new system measures skill more accurately — your rating reflects your results, not when you played.
>
> Think of it like removing grade inflation: a B is still a B, the numbers are just more honest now.

## What This Means For You

1. **Focus on your rank, not the number.** Position on the leaderboard is what matters. The raw rating is just a tool to calculate that position.

2. **No more penalty for breaks.** Taking a month off locals? Your rating won't decay. When you come back, you pick up where you left off.

3. **Historical wins matter.** That tournament you crushed six months ago? It counts just as much as last week's.

4. **The leaderboard is more stable.** Without decay constantly shifting things, positions are more meaningful and harder to game by just showing up frequently.

## Technical Details (For the Curious)

The core change is moving from a 5-pass iterative algorithm with time-based weighting to standard single-pass Elo:

- **K-factor:** Still uses provisional K (higher for new players) that decreases with experience
- **Expected score:** Standard Elo formula: `1 / (1 + 10^((Rb - Ra) / 400))`
- **Actual score:** Win = 1, Loss = 0, Tie = 0.5 (fixed from 0)
- **Processing order:** Chronological by tournament date (no iteration)
